Distillation in Ms. Fr. 640

Christopher Eom

Introduction

The entry “Thick varnish for planks,” beginning on fol. 3r, contains a schematic illustration of a distillation vessel (Fig. 1) on fol. 3v, along with a description of its ability to distill high quality turpentine and alcohol in large quantities. To understand the place of this device in the contemporaneous development of distillation technologies, this essay explores questions about where the author-practitioner obtained his information about distillation and why he was interested in it. After discussing the entry on fol. 3r-v and the accompanying illustration, I compare the distillation apparatus with other contemporaneous vessels and descriptions, and then I contextualize the entry by looking at other visual and textual representations of practical apparatuses and processes within the manuscript. I argue that it is likely the author-practitioner may have been more intent on addressing the practical needs of a distillation apparatus than on the means by which an apparatus works. One can posit that the author-practitioner himself may have learned of the vessel experientially, and thus he depicted the distillation apparatus as a practical object capable of specific outputs.

Today, distillation is defined as a process of vaporizing a liquid mixture (known as a “distilland”) by heating it in a boiling vessel or pot and condensing the vapors of a component of the mixture (known as the “distillate”) on a cool surface into a receiver. The result of this process is the collection of more volatile liquid components of the mixture in the receiver and less volatile components in the original boiling vessel. In conventional modern distillation apparatuses, the original liquid mixture is placed in the boiling flask, and once the mixture reaches the vapor temperature, volatile liquid components will vaporize and condense on a slanted condenser continuously fed by cold water on its outside, yielding the collection of this component in the receiver.1 Distillation is the standard means for the production of alcohol spirits, essential oils, herbs, and now laboratory-grade chemicals and reagents.

During the early modern period, practitioners developed these technologies and a diverse group of authors catalogued their techniques. Hieronymus Brunschwig (c. 1450 - c. 1512) authored two works on distillation, Liber de arte distillandi, de Simplicibus: das buch der rechten kunst zü distilieren die eintzigen ding (1500) and Liber de arte distillandi de compositis: das buch der waren kunst zu distillieren die Composita unn simplicia und daz Buch thesaurus pauperum (1507),2 which contained contemporary methods for constructing a distillation apparatus and performing distillations.3 One of the greatest challenges for early modern distillers was the construction of effective condensers for cooling the distillate. Brunschwig describes a “Moor’s head alembic,” similar to that depicted on fol. 3v of Ms. Fr. 640, in which he admits to not fully understanding the mechanism of circulating water to cool the vessel and illustrates a method of tapping cooling water from the bottom of the apparatus (Fig. 2).4 Conrad Gesner (c. 1516–c. 1565), whom the author-practitioner cites in a margin in Ms. Fr. 640, fol. 119v, was one of the first authors to write extensively on the use of water to cool distillates.5 His Thesaurus Evonymi Philiatri de Remediis Secretis describes various types of water-based cooling apparatuses, including a Moor’s head alembic, although none of these apparatuses illustrates a continuous cooling mechanism.6

The illustration of the distillation apparatus on fol. 3v is one of many sketches throughout the manuscript, which serve a variety of purposes. Some illustrations represent the author-practitioner’s general interests and observations.7 Others serve practical purposes, as some detailed illustrations are accompanied by comprehensive written instructions.8 The drawing of the distillation apparatus featured on fol. 3v seems to share in both of these ends. The ways in which the author-practitioner illustrates and writes about this sketch helps to elucidate aspects of his knowledge, motivations, and ultimately his identity.

Distillation Apparatus on fol. 3v

The only explicit description and visual representation of a distillation apparatus in Ms. Fr. 640 appears on fol. 3v, in the context of entries on turpentine and its use for varnishing floorboards and panel paintings. The author-practitioner notes that this copper distillation vessel is able to produce large amounts of “turpentine oil” of particular qualities. He outlines a few of the parameters under which a distillation should be undertaken, noting that the process should start with low heat, which can be done without a furnace, and that this particular distillation apparatus has a still head cooled by flowing water. He then states the price one can get for the distilled turpentine and also mentions that the colophony, called pix graeca, remains in the vessel after a distillation. He also notes that the apparatus can produce alcohol in a single distillation.

The author-practitioner’s sketch of the apparatus features the typical components of an early modern distillation apparatus:9 a cucurbit, which holds the distilland; a still head, or ambix, on which the vaporized distillate condenses; a delivery tube, or solen, protruding from the right side of the vessel, through which the condensed distillate is dispensed, and a collecting receptacle, known as the bikos (Fig. 3).10 The sketch also depicts the cooling mechanism as a basin surrounding the still head and featuring a spigot from which water heated during the distillation can be drained.

Though the author-practitioner appears to be illustrating a particular type of distillation apparatus, he describes it in the accompanying text with the simple term “vessel” (vaisseau). Use of the word vaisseau elsewhere in the manuscript shows that it is a generic term used to indicate a receptacle for holding or storing things, rather than as a means of processing materials, such as through distillation.11 Comparing the illustrated vessel to contemporary illustrations and descriptions of distillation apparatuses, it can be more or less identified as a Moor’s head alembic. This alembic featured a still head enclosed in cooling water, a technology believed to have been developed in the late fifteenth century.12 This type of apparatus is illustrated in Hieronymous Brunschwig’s text (Fig. 2).13

The author-practitioner’s sketch of the apparatus on fol. 3v closely resembles a printed image from the 1551 edition of Brunschwig’s Distillierbuch der Rechten Kunst (Fig. 4).14 Both apparatuses (Fig. 3 and Fig. 5) feature a spigot on the left of the cooling vessel surrounding the still head, which would allow water to escape the vessel. The tube leaving the right of both apparatuses may indicate the same solen where the distillate could be dispensed. Brunschwig’s vessel, like that of fol. 3v, appears to enable the distillation of alcohols, as indicated by its caption: “The common distillation vessel / for spirits and other things / now most commonly used / at the top with a copper cooling kettle15 / from which one can also draw the water off / very useful.”16 Brunschwig specifies the need for a copper condenser on top (küpfferinen Külkessel), but does not specify the composition of any other component of the vessel. This may indicate that it is the condenser which must be made from copper, which is logical, as the low specific heat of copper facilitates the cooling of the still head and the transfer of heat to the water and thus allows for efficient condensation of distillate products.

The author-practitioner, on the other hand, notes that the entire vessel should be made from copper. It is not clear whether the cucurbit or other components of the vessel were also made from copper, but his recommendation to use tinned copper (i.e., copper plated with tin) to avoid a green color in the distillate may suggest the components besides the still head would also have been made from copper. Because the still head is one of the colder parts of the apparatus (because it is cooled by water), it is less likely that reactions with the copper would occur there.17

The author-practitioner’s description of the distillation apparatus raises questions about the quantity and quality of distillate that it produced. He reports that this apparatus is able to produce turpentine oil at the rate of “a bucket an hour.” It is not clear precisely what volume a “bucket” signifies, but the author-practitioner makes clear that it is used to produce “large quantities.”18 He also describes its ability to make “fine” or “crude” turpentine though he does not specify how these different qualities are achieved through the distillation process. When writing about the distillation of alcohol in the same vessel, the author-practitioner states that “there is no need to distill it again.” This apparently speaks to the quality or purity of alcohol distillate that can be obtained with this vessel, and it seems to testify to the high efficiency achieved with this apparatus.19

The text of the entry, with its allusions to tacit knowledge (e.g., “as you know”), indicates that the author-practitioner had some familiarity with distillation techniques and expected as much from his reader. He suggests that one begin a distillation on low heat and, as Brunschwig also mentions, that one can maintain cold water within the still head throughout the process. The author-practitioner specifies the sale of turpentine distilled by his vessel, stating that it “is sold for 40 sous per lb,”20 a monetary value that is equivalent to 144 deniers or about half a pound of silver.21 This mention of the sale of the product of distillation may suggest that the author-practitioner was involved in a commercial endeavor.

Contextualization of fol. 3r-v within Ms. Fr. 640

There are diverse references to distillation, other distillation apparatuses and the products of distillation throughout Ms. Fr. 640. Chiefly, the author-practitioner mentions the use of an alembic for distillations of urine, azure and turpentine.22 The forms of the word “lembick,” “limbeck,” or the like were also used to describe the head of these distillation apparatuses from the fifteenth to seventeenth centuries, and this terminology also occurs in Ms. Fr. 640.23

In his references to alembics, the author-practitioner specifies that a copper vessel should be used. In the entry, “To relieve the pain of G.,” probably referring to gout, he writes that a distillation of imbibed urine should be done “in an alembic, well-luted & covered with a copper helmet” (fol. 7v). In yet another entry on “Painting esmail d’azur in oil,” he writes about a “rock water,” that is, “a water distilled from mines where azure or vert d’azur is found, which distills naturally through the veins of the mountain or is distilled through an alembic from mineral stones of azure or copper” (fol. 11r).

While the author-practitioner noted his interest in the market value of distilled turpentine, he was also interested in the product left over after such a distillation. The description of the distillation vessel mentions a colophony known as pix graeca, or “Greek pitch,” that remains after a distillation. Though there is never another reference within the manuscript to pix graeca directly, there is a reference to the making of colophony on fol. 3r. The author-practitioner states that it is “nothing other than recooked resin.” The rest of the entry focuses on the production of colophony, in which the author-practitioner implies that it does not directly involve any sort of distillation procedure or the like. However, given the placement of the descriptions and making of colophony on the same single folio page as the distillation entry, it seems as though the entries on colophony and on distillation complement each other. The distillation entry on fol. 3r-v may serve to explain another “origin” of the materials outlined in a previous entry on fol. 3r focusing on colophony.

Contemporary sources on colophony echo the author-practitioner’s brief remarks. Charles Estienne’s L’agriculture et maison rustique briefly makes reference to colophony as a melted substance that can be used for “diseases of the flanks” in oxen in a chapter on “the neat-heard,” or cowherd.24 Conrad Gesner refers to colophony in his Thesaurus Euonymi Philiatri de remediis secretis as a byproduct of balms intended “to close and heale wounds & biles,” including those prepared by distillation and/or with turpentine.25 These references reinforce the notion that the distillation of turpentine under heat was also a major source of colophony.

Just as we can situate the entry on fol. 3r-v within a network of information about distillation that extends throughout the manuscript, we can also compare the author-practitioner’s sketch of the distillation apparatus with his other drawings in Ms. Fr. 640. Perhaps the most notable feature about the various illustrations in the manuscript is the high variability among them. Whereas an illustration such as that found on fol. 3v is rather schematic and simple, that of fol. 54v illustrating a contraption for drying jams (Fig. 6), for instance, is extremely detailed not only its depiction of component parts, but also in the artistic attention paid to it, including the shading to illustrate liquid and the cross-hatching motif representing the sieve described in the text.

Two folios feature illustrations similar in execution to that on fol. 3v. The entry titled “Fountains” on fol. 80r describes a means by which one can raise water from a lower to higher area. This entry is similar to that of the distillation apparatus, as both involve the processing and movement of a liquid medium. Also, they both include illustrations of the apparatuses as well as textual indications of their wider practical purposes. On fol. 80r, the author-practitioner goes into great detail about how to assemble such a contraption in order to achieve this end, and this is reflected in the three illustrations devoted to the practice of feeding fountains with water (Fig 7, Fig. 8, Fig. 9). The author-practitioner demarcated certain components of one such device with letters, which suggests he could have had a construction of this contraption in mind or was copying the construction from another source. The illustrations here are similarly schematic to the diagram on fol. 3v for distillation.

Perhaps the strongest parallel to fol. 3r-v entry within Ms. Fr. 640 in terms of the subject of an illustration is that of fol. 102r. The illustration featured here is of a glass vessel which, like that on fol. 80r, is achieved with a degree of artistic finesse, as the cross-hatching and curved lines accentuate the form of the vessel (Fig. 10). However, what is most intriguing about this entry is the text associated with it. Although very simple, the glass vessel has very clearly outlined specifications, including the particular shape and a relative sense of scale with respect to easily accessible objects, such as knife handles, Dutch feathers, and fingers. Both of these features completely juxtapose the comparable features of the illustration and text of fol. 3v, in which the illustration is quite schematic and the text offers little with regards to the dimensions of the vessel, even though both illustrations concern similar subjects. However, more noteworthy perhaps are the allusions to distillation apparatuses on the same folio. Further up the page is an entry for the “Provisions for the work of Colchis,” which simply features a list of materials (fol. 102r). Among these materials are “7 retorts,”26 “6 terrines without lead,” “2 alembics for distilling vinegar.”27 Whether the illustration at the bottom of the page is correlated with the retorts alluded to further up is uncertain, though given that these retorts are the only other reference to a glass vessel on the folio, it is possible.

Ultimately, the fol. 3r-v entry and illustration appear to be observational rather than a set of instructions. It is not detailed enough for a reader to fully understand the mechanism of distillation, and thus the apparatus cannot be reconstructed on the basis of the entry alone. Rather, it is a way for the reader to understand the practical value of the apparatus and its products.

Technological Developments in Vessel Cooling Technologies

One of the most innovative aspects of the vessel depicted on fol. 3v is its cooling system. The development of a water-cooled copper still head likely began with attempts to perform distillations of alcohols and the need for precise cooling methods and heating control during distillation processes as well as for collecting a desirable yield of pure alcohol products.28 As such, a crucial aspect of proper distillation of alcohols is the means of cooling different components of a vessel in which a distillation is being done.29 The early development of an improved means of cooling targeted the solen of an apparatus using wet sponges or rags.30 The development of more robust glasses and their consequent popularity as a material for distillation vessels in turn allowed for further explorations into more efficient cooling methods.31 Taddeo Alderotti (d. 1295) writes of a cooling method in the late thirteenth century in which a tub of water was built around an elongated, arm-length solen, believed to be in a worm-like shape (canalem serpentinum), to further cool the delivery tube.32

Later, innovations in distillation technologies focused on the cooling of the still head rather than of the solen, using more continuous and rigorous cooling methods than the replaceable sponges or wet rags used previously. This, in conjunction with the need to collect distillates in fraction with a low boiling point, resulted in the development of the Rosenhut and the “Moor’s head.” The Rosenhut, the earlier of the two alembics and observed in a fully fleshed out form by 1478, was a conical rimmed alembic sitting on a wide-mouthed cucurbit and often made from metals because of their high thermal conductivity for efficient cooling by air.33 The prominence of the Rosenhut in the sixteenth century is apparent as illustrated in the title page (Fig. 11) and seventh plate (Fig. 12) of the Nova Reperta, a print series engraved by Jan Collaert I (c. 1525/30 – 1580) after Jan van der Straet (1523–1625).34 However, neither of these illustrations indicate the use of water-based cooling mechanisms for distillation apparatuses, as is provided by the Moor’s head alembic and the apparatus illustrated on fol. 3v. Thus, the inclusion of the apparatus pictured on fol. 3v, as the author-practitioner clearly recognizes, stems from advances in cooling technologies associated with distillation vessels.

Distillation Practices Contemporaneous with Ms. Fr. 640

When considering larger-scale production and sale of distilled products, apothecaries appear to have played a considerable role from the late twelfth century and, to a lesser extent, vintners and innkeepers.35 Furthermore, in France specifically, the distillers trade was controlled by the state from 1619. Distillation remained a craft reserved for apothecaries and spice-merchants until 1624 and 1634, when statutes were implemented for distillers to be placed within the apothecary guild. The distillers gained their own guild in 1637.36

The distillation of essential oils was mostly restricted to monasteries, especially those that had herb gardens, although by the fourteenth century, the plants for essential oils, such as lavender, could be grown on large plantations, such as those in Burgundy. These plants were distilled with water or alcohol in a high-throughput manner, using many stills.

Where did the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 gain his knowledge of the distillation apparatus on fol. 3v? The most extensive works on distillation practices at this time were those of Hieronymus Brunschwig. As noted above, Brunschwig’s Distillierbuch der rechten kunst depicts a distillation apparatus closely resembling the vessel on fol. 3v. At the time of the writing of Ms. Fr. 640, it appears Brunschwig’s works were not translated or readily available in French, thus making it less likely that the author-practitioner would have read his works. This does not preclude the author-practitioner having knowledge of Brunschwig and his apparatuses in German, as the author-practitioner was familiar with German materials and practices.37 Conrad Gesner, whose “de lapidibus” is mentioned in a margin of Ms. Fr. 640, also discusses distillation,38 but the closest source of distillation practices to the author-practitioner was Charles Estienne’s Maison Rustique. It highlights several characteristics of distilling alcohols, essential oils and other forms of liquid products. Estienne outlines the ways in which aqua vitae is distilled, describing the vessels and procedures, aiming, like the description on fol. 3v, for high yield. He also describes the consequences of multiple distillations, including the relative amount of product and quality of the distillate. He likewise uses the same unit of “bucket” as a means of quantification of product yield. Finally, and perhaps most notably, Estienne includes details about the distillation vessels, including those made from copper.39 One particular illustration closely resembles the alembic found in Ms. Fr. 640, as it depicts what appears to be a water-cooled still head with the same spigot and collection components illustrated by the author-practitioner (Fig. 13). Since this vessel also closely resembles the common distillation vessel of Brunschwig, it is probable that Estienne drew inspiration from Brunschwig’s work, and in turn it is possible that the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640 may have drawn inspiration from Estienne’s work.

Conclusion

Distillation was of great interest during the early modern period, and it is not surprising to find that the author-practitioner was aware of and showed interest in the process. This essay has contextualized his description and visual representation of the distillation process using a Moor’s head alembic with a water-cooled still head. Brunschwig’s nearly identical vessel in the Distillierbuch der Rechten Kunst, or Estienne’s description may have been the basis of the sketch on fol. 3v, or the author-practitioner might simply have observed such vessels being used. Neither the author-practitioner’s description of the vessel nor the accompanying drawing provide enough details for a reader to reconstruct the apparatus, and thus the author-practitioner’s interest appears to have been in practical processes and the products that could be produced with relatively novel technologies—namely, with a water-cooled copper condenser. His apparent interest in the commercial aspects of these distilled products may likewise prove significant for interpreting other sections of the manuscript. Ultimately, his interest in distillation appears not unlike his interest in many other processes described throughout the manuscript, that is, motivated by practical uses and interest in the means of transforming materials.
