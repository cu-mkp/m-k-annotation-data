Imitating Raw Nature

Isabella Lores-Chavez

If imitation is the sincerest form of flattery, then early modern artisans were demonstrably preoccupied with paying material compliments to nature. In Ms. Fr. 640, among the profusion of recipes for making curious objects in the workshop, the author-practitioner includes several entries concerned with the imitation of raw natural materials. This is a distinct subcategory within the manuscript, for, instead of providing instructions for creating objects, these recipes aim to produce a manmade double for a naturally occurring material or object. The use in these texts of a specific language of imitation—most notably, the use of the term contrefaire to designate such doubles—further distinguishes these entries. This essay will investigate how the author-practitioner undertakes and articulates the imitation of natural materials, and it will catalogue the individual terms used to describe the reproduction of the natural. As a case study, we reconstruct the recipe for making jasper out of bovine horn on fol. 10r. With insights from the physical experience of making imitation jasper, we consider the implications (and limitations) of artificial materials standing in for materials found in nature.1

Among the many questions that arise from the manuscript’s preoccupation with imitation is how to determine the ultimate goal of “material mimesis,” as Marjolijn Bol has phrased it. The purpose of the author-practitioner’s imitations is not always clear, which suggests that his intentions were to create products that substituted for or counterfeited their originals in diverse ways. In his attention to the color and surface properties of the substances to be reproduced, the author-practitioner describes precisely the phenomenon that Bol calls material mimesis, where one material imitates the visual properties of another.2 Beyond coming to an understanding of the author-practitioner’s language in this endeavor, this essay explores his strategies for use and display that could augment an imitation’s verisimilitude. Although physical likeness was the primary objective, our reconstruction suggests that the process of imitating or reproducing a material might also have helped the author-practitioner understand both it and the forces behind its generation in nature.

We use the term “raw material” here to designate naturally occurring substances that have not undergone any kind of intervention or transformation by human hands or tools. Taking cues from the manuscript, we limit our focus to jasper (and similar stones), coral and wood.3 We will not treat the manuscript’s techniques for casting from life, as this process has been researched elsewhere and represents a separate class of imitation.4 We explore what constituted a convincing imitation of naturalia prized by early modern collectors, thinkers and artisans, and consider the status and epistemic aims of these products within collections of the time.

Coming to Terms with Imitation

Ms. Fr. 640 contains a rich array of terms used by the author-practitioner for the difficult task of translating physical experience and knowledge into textual accounts. His terms for imitative practices are deliberate and, at times, precise. A search of the manuscript for words or roots potentially associated with mimesis yields natur-, contref- (from contrefaire, or “to counterfeit”), imit-, selon, vray, aproch-, artific- and effect. A summary of the incidence of these terms in Ms. Fr. 640 can be found in Appendix I (at the end of this essay). Several noteworthy points emerged from our search: first, the author-practitioner employs the term le naturel (appearing thirty-seven times) to refer to the natural state of a thing. This term—literally, “the natural”—suggests a concept in which the natural properties of a material or creature exist as a kind of independent component within it. By contrast, the use of nature with a possessive—“its nature”—occurs infrequently. For example, in fol. 105v, the author-practitioner describes methods for catching nightingales and emphasizes that il faut observer leur naturel. Rather than recommending observing nightingales in nature or noting their natural habits, he attributes “the natural” to the creatures themselves. In other words, the behaviors of physical beings or materials are not described by reference to natural tendencies or inclinations but rather by an acknowledgement of the natural that resides within them. This is arguably akin to what Paracelsus understood as the scientia of each of nature’s creations.5 Second, the author-practitioner’s interest in observing how le naturel manifests itself is made clear by his pairing of the term with selon (“according to”) or, relatedly, comme (“as”). Of the thirty-seven times he uses le naturel, it appears after selon or comme a total of twelve times. When describing the casting of a rose on fol. 155v, the author-practitioner insists on the importance of painting it selon le naturel, imparting to “the natural” an individual authority from which the maker may take his cue.

Consistently, le naturel appears in recipes that deal with tips for either visual representation or physical manipulation (whether referring to a material’s behavior in an unfamiliar process or indicating what to expect from handling a substance, including animals for molding). The recurring presence of terms related to the word “nature” (a total of fifty-nine occurrences) attests to the author-practitioner’s preoccupation with understanding the raw materials with which he claims to work. Notably, the author-practitioner does not have an equivalent for our modern term “naturalism.” His terms related to nature are predominantly about the physical properties of things; these terms emphasize the value of the physical object as the starting point for faithful representation.6 The author-practitioner also uses the term le naturel to refer to the natural object itself, often to insist on the importance of firsthand observation. In his marginal note on fol. 141v about painting molded crayfish to augment their lifelikeness, le naturel is presented as the unmatched source of visual information that guides the artisan’s work: “As in this & all other things, have always the natural one in front of you to imitate it” (fol. 141v).7

The most intriguing insight into his conception of mimesis emerges from a comparison of his use of imiter (“imitate”) and contrefaire (“counterfeit”). Imiter and its variants appear a total of twelve times in the manuscript, in the context of both two- and three-dimensional representations. Contrefaire, on the other hand, appears seven times, with all but one instance (for imitating roses with horn scrapings on fol. 10r) about producing doubles for raw materials. Given his apparent differentiation between these terms, contrefaire may thus specifically denote material mimesis in the manuscript. This particularly applies to the recipe on fol. 10r for Jaspe contrefaict. We might translate this as “Imitation Jasper,” but the distinction between imiter and contrefaire suggests that “Counterfeit Jasper” would not be a misnomer, at least from the perspective of the sixteenth-century reader (as discussed in the next section). The author uses contrefaire as an adjective modifying jasper in the recipe title, and then again as a verb in the long marginal note in which he writes, “Pour mieulx contrefaire jaspe grumeleux” (“To better counterfeit mottled jasper”). This is in contrast to the meaning of imiter within the same recipe that he uses in his inventive aside about imitating a rose using the scrapings from the horn used for the jasper.8 “Les roses se peuvent imiter” (“you can imitate roses”) certainly delights in the possibility of visually recalling a rose by gathering horn scrapings. This itself is a kind of material mimesis: the delicate curls of the horn shavings make them resemble the petals of a rose, which is perhaps why he goes on in another recipe on 10r entitled “Roses” to say that these are “counterfeited” by dyeing the scrapings of horn or parchment. The contrast between contrefaire for jasper and imiter for roses in a single recipe, however, alerts us to a distinction that the author-practitioner appears to make between a visual similarity and a feasible visual substitution for the raw material. If we are to take him at his word, jaspe contrefaict is so materially close to the original stone that it could stand in for it, rather than merely appearing like it.

Portrait of the Counterfeit

In the early modern period, the term contrafactum and its vernacular cognates emerged as a powerful way to convey authenticity and “fact” by means of the visual. Art historian Peter Parshall has shown how this term served as a label “most often employed within the emerging genres of portraiture and topography, for images reporting specific events, and for portrayals of both natural and preternatural phenomena.”9 To characterize an image as a counterfeit had entirely different implications than it does today, describing not a mode of deception but rather an attempt to communicate firsthand or eyewitness information. The fact that it was most often applied to portraits reveals that the term could convey both the image maker’s contact with the “original” and also the effectiveness of the representation itself. An imago contrafacta indeed stood in for the real person, event, or object it described, but not in order to deceive. The term contrafactum could support “subjects that for one reason or another seemed to ask for a special order of legitimacy”10 precisely because they inspired wonder and disbelief. The media revolution that instigated the wide circulation of prints helped disseminate and reinforce the use of the term. The increasingly active “rhetoric of verifiability and actuality” in the early modern period “granted images so described a certain truth value, one not always in sync with the reality effect of the image itself.”11 The author-practitioner’s use of contrefaire in the manuscript appears to be congruent with this usage and suggests that he intends his “counterfeit” materials to convey an unmediated extension of nature. Using this term to describe a representation of a natural material, he appears to claim that legitimate knowledge of the original derived from material imitation can be independent from the persuasiveness of the representation. In other words, imitating can lead to knowing, even when the imitation produced is not fully convincing.

Other authors use the term contrefaire to similar ends. Giovanni Villani’s “Segreti” uses the adjective contrafatti in recipes devoted to imitating valuable raw materials, namely amber, ivory, coral, and pearls.12 The pseudonymous Alessio Piemontese’s popular book of secrets (De Secreti, 1555), like Ms. Fr. 640, provides recipes for artistic techniques and experiments with material mimesis, and uses contrafatto in a recipe for making ebony “so beautiful as to cause shame to the natural.”13 A French edition of the book, printed in 1627, includes a recipe for making imitation pearls: “Pour contrefaire perles lesquelles seront forts belles comme naturelles.”14 Making objects that resembled these raw materials seems to have been understood specifically as a process of taking direct cues from nature to convey the appearance of the materials’ natural state, with the implication that the finished products were visually close enough to the original materials that they could function in their place. The “counterfeit” could be a visual substitute for the thing itself, much like a portrait—the ultimate imago contrafacta—could be valued as a stand-in without being confused for the actual person.15 If the author-practitioner understood his manufactured jasper to be more contrefactum than mere imitation, we may further consider how his jaspe contrefaict might function as a representation charged with conveying not only appearance but even knowledge of the “nature” or essence of jasper.

(Re)turning to Jasper

Reconstruction of the fol. 10r recipe for counterfeiting jasper allowed us to investigate the feasibility of the author-practitioner’s proposed material mimesis.16 For this essay, we referred to the previous reconstruction, “Jasper Imitation on Horn,” by Ana Estrades and Wenrui Zhao. We worked with the same type of water buffalo horn that formed the basis of their lab research.17 We used a wood planer and sandpaper to plane down a sheet of horn until it was thin enough to be translucent (from about 5 mm to about 3 mm). We then cut the horn into five rectangular pieces (about 5 cm long) using a jeweler’s saw (Fig. 1). For our first attempt at painting the horn, we mixed green and red paints, using the pigments verdigris and madder lake, respectively, and linseed oil as the base. We opted for the medium of oil rather than turpentine or spike lavender varnish as called for in the recipe, in order to test the effects of the paint on the horn using a medium that would yield relatively translucent glazes.18 Using three pieces of horn, we painted one piece green, one piece red, and one piece with alternating diagonal strokes of red and green in order to evoke the oblique bands sometimes visible on jasper (Fig. 2).

For this reconstruction, we decided to test the additional recommendation the author-practitioner makes in a marginal note, where he indicates that a better “counterfeit” mottled jasper can be made using “laines a gros poils tainctes de diverses couleurs” (at first translated as, “heavy yarn dyed various colors”).19 This passage is not explicit about how the yarn ought to be colored, nor does it indicate how to adhere the yarn to the painted horn. Prior to painting the horn, we dyed two samples of yarn made of 100% sheep’s wool in the lab, using alum as a mordant to ensure the yarn would be dyed a bright color (Fig. 3).20 During our first painting trial, we had strands of the red yarn ready to press onto the horn while the paint was still wet. Because the author-practitioner does not specify any kind of adhesive, we decided first to try pressing the yarn into the oil paint to see if it would naturally stick (Fig. 4). When this failed, we attempted an alternative method with another piece of the horn, dipping the yarn into the paint and then using the paint-soaked yarn to spread paint across the surface of the horn. To compare the horn with and without application of the yarn, we ran strands of the red yarn dipped in red paint across the surface of only half of each piece of horn (Fig. 5). This technique resulted in a slightly marbled, less uniform look to the surface that had been painted with the yarn. Held up to the light and viewed from the unpainted side, the horn did bear some resemblance to a lustrous stone (Fig. 6).

For our second trial, we used spike lavender varnish as the medium for green and red paint, again made from verdigris and madder lake respectively.21 We painted our remaining two pieces of horn with the varnish colors, this time suspecting that the red yarn would stick readily to the new medium. When pressure failed to adhere the yarn to the horn, we decided to dip it in the varnish before applying it to the horn. This strand of yarn, applied to the red-painted sheet, certainly stuck more effectively, but it still did not achieve a marbled effect as suggested by the author-practitioner (Fig. 7).

For all five samples of painted horn, we used a sharp metal tool to scrape oblique lines into the horn after the colors had been laid down, following the recipe instructions. Finally, we applied gold leaf (transfer leaf) to half of each painted piece of horn and oiled the unpainted side with spike lavender oil. These last three steps contributed significantly to the effect: the scratched lines created delicate patterns not unlike those found in real jasper, while the gold backing brightened the color of the painted horn and highlighted the fine scratched lines further. Oiling the unpainted side with spike lavender oil rendered the horn immediately more translucent, making the painted side more visible, while helping the horn retain the “luster & fatty polish like jasper.” (“un lustre & polyment gras comme le jaspe”) that made it the author-practitioner’s material of choice (Fig. 8).

Reconstruction Insights

Reconstructing the recipe on fol. 10r made clear how the author-practitioner may have experimented with various materials and techniques for making imitation jasper. After multiple trials, we could not fully ascertain what the author-practitioner intended by his marginal note instructing the use of yarn or wool to better “counterfeit” jasper: “pour

mieulx contrefaire/ jaspe grumeleux/ aplique des laines/ a gros poil tainctes/ de diverses couleurs/ & entresmeslees” (“To better counterfeit mottled jasper, apply wool with thick hairs dyed in diverse colors & intermingled”). This is partly due to the note’s language: aplique des laines is ambiguous and does not specify how to apply the material, while tainctes de diverses couleurs could refer to dyed yarn or simply to wool dipped in paint. Our reconstructions using dyed yarn were not very compelling; indeed, our most convincing samples resulted from either omitting this step (Fig. 9) or dipping the yarn in paint and dragging it across the painted surface (Fig. 10). Piemontese uses the verb taindre repeatedly in his book of secrets in recipes for coloring horn and wood, clearly indicating a process of dyeing.22 If the author-practitioner is indeed referring to dyed wool, he neglects to describe how to attach it to the horn. The term aplique does not seem to carry the same implications as the term couche in the recipe, which denotes laying a material onto a surface (the colors and the gold leaf, for example). Further reconstructions might shed light on this step, especially if they take into consideration that laine à gros poil most likely refers to coarse wool rather than spun yarn.23

The remaining instructions for imitating jasper were much more practicable. The layout of the text on fol. 10r provides compelling evidence for the author-practitioner’s possible sequence of experimentation. The marginal note, which communicates variations on the process, as well as possible uses of the product, extends far down the left side of folio (Fig. 11). It includes the first tip for making the imitation more convincing: the use of colored wool. The placement of this technique in the marginal note may indicate that the author-practitioner tested it at a later point, or perhaps that he obtained it elsewhere and did not try it himself. Directly following this advice are instructions for scratching oblique lines and backing with gold leaf, both of which significantly augmented the appearance of the horn as a patterned yet translucent object. The use of wool and the scratching followed by gilding seem to be distinct techniques he employed in later trials, or ideas he wrote down for later testing.

In contrast, the final step of oiling the unpainted side, which certainly contributed to the product’s verisimilitude, appears urgently squeezed in at the end of the main body of the recipe, on either side of the heading for the following recipe (Fig. 12). The author-practitioner had apparently written the main steps but soon thereafter chose to include the oiling in the main body of the text, not as a marginal aside. This suggests that the author-practitioner knew of its effectiveness even in his initial attempts.24 Based on our reconstruction, it seems likely that the author-practitioner attempted this recipe multiple times. Several more trials would certainly lead to more convincing results than those we achieved on the first try.

Both the analysis of the author-practitioner’s language and our reconstruction make a case for his awareness of the value of his own artifice. The purpose of making imitation jasper was not necessarily to pass it off as the real thing, but instead perhaps to convey the “essence” of the material and to understand how the real stone came to have that essence and appearance. This aim could explain the possible variations in the recipe. The objective thus was not to create a material mimesis that could potentially deceive but rather to “explain the physical problem of the genesis of nature’s materials,” comparable to Albertus Magnus’s belief “that nature could be understood from the direct analogy to its artisanal counterparts.”25 To examine whether this applies to the author-practitioner’s process of making jasper, we must consider both the function of jasper in the early modern period and the epistemological dimensions of his recipe.

The Place and Purpose of Jasper

In the sixteenth century, jasper was among several semi-precious stones that were commonly collected in princely Kunstkammern and even in modest cabinets of curiosity. Already in the medieval period, the taste for jasper existed at the imperial courts of Prague, where, in the fourteenth century, Charles IV collected fine cups and bowls carved from the locally mined stone (Fig. 13).26 Generations later, Rudolf II continued to treasure bejeweled vessels made of Bohemian jasper, displaying them in the capacious theatrum mundi he had famously amassed.27 Around 1597, the Medici grand-ducal workshops in Prague were in the process of making a round table (now lost) of jasper and porphyry for him.28 Additionally, he commissioned large tabletops in the commesso di pietre dure technique from Cosimo Castrucci and his workshop in Prague.29 These compositions (Fig. 14) combine several different kinds of stones, including jasper, subsuming the characteristics of each stone into the overall effect of the colorful landscape. Here, jasper as an individual stone matters less than the surface effects it contributes to the whole.

At Schloss Ambras, in the extensive Kunstkammer of Archduke Ferdinand II, jasper was among the gemstones and minerals included in the archduke’s collection of naturalia in their raw form, which were evidently sometimes displayed alongside artistically worked versions of the same materials.30 The archduke’s collection included a seal signet outfitted with a large jasper handle, an object that permitted the simultaneous appreciation and use of the stone (Fig. 15). In the seventeenth century, Emperor Ferdinand III owned a vase by Dionysio Miseroni with stone flowers by Paul Pertz, which employed jasper as one of several stones carved into the shape of a flower (Fig. 16). The work of the sculptor, who carved the jasper into the shape of a tulip, embellishes the stone’s natural preciousness; its value derives from a combination of the raw material and the sculptor’s artifice. In the early modern Kunstkammer, jasper was thus appreciated both as a marvelous stone and as a workable raw material. Jasper could also be admired for receiving images directly from nature. In the 1644 edition of Le parfaict joaillier, Anselm de Boodt discusses the specific hardness of jasper and the ways sculptors worked it. When comparing heliotrope to jasper, he remarks that it has “une plus grande auctorité que le iaspe, si ce n’est qu’à ce iaspe la nature ayt imprimée des images & effigies.”31 De Boodt indicates how figuration naturally occurring in jasper could increase its value, simultaneously drawing an analogy between the work of nature and the sculptor’s carving of images into the stone.

The author-practitioner undoubtedly had opportunities to see jasper, given its availability in European mines. He was certainly aware of its possible uses as more than a curious stone: the long marginal note squeezed onto fol. 10r begins with an explanation of how to inlay bed frames using imitation jasper.32 This decorative context of a piece of imitation jasper could serve to augment its verisimilitude: when not the sole focus of the viewer’s attention, its even modest jasper-like visual qualities could be sufficiently effective. The fact that the fol. 10r recipe additionally mentions carnelian and other stones among the materials to “counterfeit” suggests that the author-practitioner had firsthand knowledge of chalcedony in general.

The author-practitioner may have been familiar with the writings of Bernard Palissy, who made ceramic casts from life using techniques comparable to the author-practitioner’s life castings in metal. In 1580, Palissy published his lectures on natural history in a volume called Discours admirables (Admirable Discourses). Among the many descriptions Palissy provides for the formation and properties of natural materials, there is a long section specifically on rocks, which includes detailed explanations for the physical appearance of stones like jasper. He is particularly preoccupied with these stones’ coloring:

As for the colors of figured marbles, jaspers, porphyries, serpentines, and other such kinds, their colors are caused by various drips of water that fall from the surface of the earth to the place where these rocks are formed: the waters coming from many and various places in the earth, in descending bring with them these various colors which are in these stones… Other drops will carry other various colors that will cause many patterns on the rocks.33

Palissy pictures multiple steps in the formation of rocks deep within the earth, positing that the final colors and textures of jasper were generated by the movement of water.34 The author-practitioner’s recipe for jaspe contrefaict shows a similar interest in the coloring and patterning of the stone, perhaps indicating an inquisitiveness comparable to that which spurred Palissy’s theories about the origins of such stones. The author-practitioner experiments with—perhaps invents—techniques for materially imitating the appearance of jasper as well as for “generating” it. He specifically considers how colors ought to be laid down but also disrupted and moved around (with yarn or by scraping). The admonition not to use opaque colors also suggests a concept of colors first suspended in water and then made solid and visible by their union with a substrate.35 The author-practitioner’s encounter with the real stone could have prompted questions about how nature produces such astonishing visual phenomena and also informed his notions of how he could materially mimic those effects.

The Universal Class

In both Ms. Fr. 640 and in the Kunstkammern of early modern Europe, jasper coexisted with several other materials sought after in their raw form. Coral and multi-colored woods were essential parts of any stock of naturalia. Although each of these, too, was incorporated into larger vessels and artificialia, the natural appearance was itself a source of fascination for collectors and artisans alike. Ms. Fr. 640 contains several recipes for making colored wood, among them the general “Recipe for putting all woods in color” (fols. 75v–76r) as well as “For making wood green ,” “For making wood red,” and even “For making it violet” (all on fol. 76r). Wood of varying colors could be found naturally in Europe and was imported from overseas; this was the essential material in techniques like the intarsia used for Duke Federico da Montafeltro’s studiolo, designed by Francesco di Giorgio Martini between 1478 and 1482.36 The author-practitioner’s inclusion of techniques for dyeing wood accords with his general interest in the manuscript in decorative practices involving painted materials, including horn.37 Colored wood on its own could also feature among the naturalia collected in Kunstkammern. Judging from his comments about the potential uses of the jaspe contrefaict, it is likely that the author-practitioner had both uses of colored wood in mind when he included these recipes in the manuscript, allowing for an appreciation of the aesthetic qualities as well as the potential decorative applications of both the raw material and its imitation.

As with jasper, the author-practitioner provides instructions for making coral using the designation coral contrefaict, on fol. 3r. This recipe suggests a material mimesis so successful that it could substitute for the precious original; even the marginal note reiterates the verb contrefaire rather than using imiter.38 Coral was a favorite collector’s item in early modern Kunstkammern for its visual appeal, its restorative properties, and its suitability for carving. Ferdinand II included it in his Schloss Ambras collection as a raw material, as a decorative addition to mounting for exotica like ostrich eggs, and as an eye-catching component in his intricate lapides manuales (Fig. 17). The author-practitioner’s recipe produces a version of coral that looks like natural coral; the reconstruction made by the Making and Knowing team with Dr. Elisabeth Berry Drago attests to the convincing appearance of the imitation (Fig. 18). For some collectors, coral’s visual appeal coincided with an appreciation for the knowledge that close inspection could yield. Ferrante Imperato, the Neapolitan apothecary, relied on direct observation of “the materials in his collections to describe anew the contours of the natural world” while preparing his 1599 Dell’Historia Naturale.39 Imperato discusses the physical properties of coral and explains how he thinks it is made, based on close examination of actual specimens. He believed that coral grew over branches, and sees proof of this in having found what he thought were natural coral specimens that, when broken open, exposed wood cores.40 Unbeknownst to Imperato, he was probably looking at a piece of imitation coral.

Samuel Quiccheberg’s 1565 treatise Inscriptiones Tituli Theatri Amplissimi sheds light on some of the conceptual frameworks that resulted in jasper, wood and coral all taking such a variety of forms in the cabinets of curiosity of early modern Europe. These raw materials belong to what Quiccheberg calls “the universal class … compri[sing] natural things and the entirety of natural matter.”41 In Quiccheberg’s system for organizing objects in a collection, naturalia belonged to the third of five classes, within which there were eleven “inscriptions,” or sub-categories. Remarkably, several inscriptions in the third class encourage the possession of materials taken directly from nature or “imitated by artifice.”42 All within the same inscription, Quiccheberg includes “herbs, flowers, small branches, boughs, pieces of bark, wood, roots, and the like” as well as versions of these “either cast from metal of some kind, woven from silk, or depicted by some new art.” Furthermore, the “earthen materials and liquids” in the eleventh inscription within this class can be “both naturally produced and manufactured.”43 In Quiccheberg’s conception of a collection of naturalia, there is potential for a material equivalence between the raw material and its imitation. By including man-made objects created by techniques unknown to him within the same inscription as natural materials, he demonstrates that he considers an imitation not to be a degraded product but rather an ingenious complement to the raw material.

Such indications in Quiccheberg’s treatise make clear that imitations of raw nature were not considered second-rate in the Kunstkammer, the workshop, or the theoretical texts that sometimes linked them. “Reproduction was an achievement in itself, and those objects were accorded a value sui generis,” and they were presented as a welcome opportunity to compare natural artifice to human artistry.44 Beyond this playful paragone, the resemblance between the raw material and the imago contrefacta (whether an image or an object) could promote an understanding of the natural processes that engendered such visually impressive substances. These projects in material mimesis were both artful endeavors and investigations of nature. Possessing both the imitation and the natural material, a collector might have considered “that art could, by virtue of the knowledge about natural processes on the part of the artist, produce things that were “virtually” natural.”45 The artisan, meanwhile, moved from a close examination of the raw material to its re-creation in the workshop, using familiar tools and techniques to make objects that could rival nature’s impressive artifice.

Making and Owning

Quiccheberg’s Inscriptiones seems to make room, in a complete theater mundi, for precisely the kind of imitative techniques that the author-practitioner proposes in Ms. Fr. 640. However, the specific motivations for these endeavors remain multifold and at times elusive. If the products of such experimentation could serve as actual substitutes for the original raw material, could there have been a real potential for deception, in the sense of a modern forgery?46 Given the commercial success of exotica and natural materials sought after for Kunstkammern, charlatans certainly abounded, “not aspir[ing] to interpret nature, but to sell nature to those who created knowledge out of the raw ingredients of the marketplace.”47 The author-practitioner probably belongs to the category of those who created knowledge rather than selling deceptions. Despite its rich use of imitation terms, the recipe on fol. 10r considers the practical, not the commercial, aspects of the making and the product itself. The author-practitioner’s jaspe contrefaict was not destined to garner a high price and enter a princely Kunstkammer, but it could instead serve a welcome decorative purpose for a reader-practitioner without the personal wealth to procure real jasper. The promise of its adjective—contrefaict—could be one of substitution as an alternative to owning the real thing.

In the lively epistemic space of the Kunstkammer, ownership of the contrefaict—be it jasper, coral, or wood—could represent both an intimate knowledge of nature and an astute appreciation of artisanal skills. The intermingling of naturalia and artificialia posing as naturalia serves as a reminder that, in imitating the artifice of nature, artisans had the paradoxical task of effacing traces of their making processes in celebrating their own art. In this sense, the imitation perhaps ought not be so convincing as to completely elide the ingenuity of its maker. Ultimately, the inevitable homology between nature’s creations and the artisan’s own made imitation or counterfeiting constituted a valuable mode of creating knowledge about nature in this period. In his imitation recipes, the author-practitioner articulates a practice that was undertaken on a daily basis in the artisan’s workshop: through physical manipulation, certain materials could be pushed to undergo a transformation to resemble another material.48 Through an entirely artificial process of becoming, one material could take on the visual appearance—and even the physical properties—of another. The distinction the author-practitioner seems to make between imiter and contrefaire indicates his subtle understanding of how workshop versions of coveted raw materials could be compelling, if not perfectly convincing, substitutes.

The author-practitioner’s trials in material mimesis were likely based on firsthand knowledge of raw substances themselves. His experimentation with imitation could present opportunities to inquire about natural phenomena. Meanwhile, the enthusiasm on the part of intellectuals like Quiccheberg for nature “depicted by some new art” surely spurred artisanal practices of imitation. The endeavor to create a manmade equivalent could prompt the artisan to study the peculiarities of the raw material and to seek practicable techniques that could match (without supplanting) nature’s creative forces.

Stones such as jasper sustained both visual and scientific appeal in the early modern period; they showed distinctive patterning, but no two specimens were ever quite the same. For the author-practitioner, owning and examining this kind of object up close seems to have raised questions about nature’s processes of making, which in turn prompted his interest in recreating its effects. Jasper’s material idiosyncrasies in many ways invite the variations of the artisan’s technique, each trial different than the next. Those same irregularities prompted inquiries as much for the author-practitioner as for his contemporary naturalists. In the catalogue of his own Kunstkammer, Ferrante Imperato, according to Paula Findlen, “argued that nature’s causes did not exceed the powers of ordinary observers” and “underscored nature as a knowable entity, full of ordinary rather than magical wonder.”49 Discovering how ordinary materials can produce the verisimilitude of other materials is central to the author-practitioner’s endeavors. His ingenious attempt to “turn” bovine horn into jasper is a far cry from Bernard Palissy’s description of water slipping color onto primordial clay before it congeals to form patterned stones like jasper.50 Yet in a similar fashion, the recipe communicates a curiosity about the ways that nature creates and astonishes, engendering improbable transformations through which water can become color and soft earth can become durable stone. The author-practitioner’s own processes begin in readily accessible workshop materials, combined in what sometimes appear to be implausible ways, yet they, too, can lead to transformative creations.
