Abstract

This essay explores a set of seventy-five manuscripts that belonged to Philippe de Béthune, now in the Bibliothèque nationale de France, containing encyclopedic knowledge and writings on natural philosophy and alchemy (Ms. Fr. 2513-2589). The themes reflected in these manuscripts give insight into Philippe de Béthune’s interests and help situate Ms. Fr. 640 within them. Although some of these manuscripts touch on similar themes to Ms. Fr. 640, and others treat observations about materials and information related to artisanal techniques, none display the compilation of practical recipes that characterizes Ms. Fr. 640.

In December 1662, when Hippolyte de Béthune offered to Louis XIV the collection of books, paintings, and statues that he had assembled with his father Philippe over their lives, it included about 2000 manuscripts.1 Suzanne Solente first focused on classifying these manuscripts thematically,2 then Didier Kahn put forward a set of seventy-five manuscripts compiling encyclopedic knowledge and writings on natural philosophy and alchemy (BnF Mss. Français 2513–2589), which are the subject of this essay.3 However, these manuscripts are not the only ones focusing on alchemy in the Béthune collection. Other manuscripts (e.g., BnF Mss. Français 641 and Latin 7180) also exhibit an interest in the transformation of materials and in gold, and are discussed by Colin Debuiche in his essay in this Edition.4

Philosophy, Literature, and the Art of Discourse

Among the manuscripts highlighted by Kahn, many deal with rhetoric, eloquence, discourse and the French language.5 Several are compilations of ancient writings on philosophy or nature, such as Plato’s Dialogues or the texts of the mythical Hermes Trismegistus.6 Other manuscripts bring together ancient and modern writings on philosophy, sometimes mixed with discourses on eloquence.7 Some of these manuscripts also contain extracts on politics and war, with reflections on morality and philosophical notes by ancient and modern authors.8

Some ancient and modern authors appear repeatedly in this collection of manuscripts. One is Raymond Lull, a Christian Catalan philosopher of the thirteenth century. Alongside several extracts on theology, his texts appearing in the collection are Logique, Fruitz de l’arbre, L’Art admirable, Les Articles de la foy chrestienne, and Traicté de la phisicque.9 From the fourteenth century on, several texts dealing with alchemy were published under his name although he rejected this science in his lifetime. Some of these texts, then, are only attributed to him. Other authors who were contemporaries of Philippe de Béthune are also well represented, including the English philosopher and scholar Francis Bacon, some of whose writings appear in three compilations,10 and the Brabant humanist and theologian Justus Lipsius, excerpts by whom are found in two manuscripts.11 Many other seventeenth-century texts, including De la Logique, Regle d’argumenter belle et tres facile, De la Science, Politique et economique, and various other excerpts, are authored by a “de La Martelaye,” who appears to have been a French philosopher unknown to historiography.12

Alchemy

Several manuscripts contain texts on alchemy (or “chemistry,” since the two terms were used interchangeably to refer to the same knowledge and practices).13 More particularly, Ms. Français 2528 demonstrates the Béthunes’ interest in this field: it contains a text entitled Considérations de la chimie qui descouvre et fait veoir la verité qui est en ses opperations, written by an “M. Dubois,” and two other texts on Chimie and Apologie de la chimie, both written by de La Tourete. In Ms. Français 2529, another figure, “M. Bossard,” who is said to be from Poitiers, is named as the author of this text entitled De la Chymie.

In addition, parts of the collection show an interest in the Kabbalah, which was often associated with alchemy.14 Ms. Français 2530 contains, among excerpts from Lull, Bacon, and writings on theology and medicine, a text entitled L’Œuvre cabalistic. Similarly, Ms. Français 2533 contains writings on medicine and a text entitled De la Caballe. Ms. Français 2540 contains extracts from Raymond Lull and others on theology and medicine, as well as a text entitled Caballe, alongside another, written by Jean Pontan, dealing with La Pierre philosophale. This author said that he had “travelled through several regions of the Earth in order to [...] acquire some knowledge of the stone popularly known as the Philosophers’ Stone.”15 In it, he relates his experiences with and reflections on the transformation of matter.

Some excerpts contained in the manuscripts focus on metals and ingredients used by the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640.16 Thus, Ms. Français 2528 and Ms. Français 2553 deal with salt, a subject linked to chemical philosophy, in a section entitled Du Sel et de l’esprit du monde (Of salt and the spirit of the world) by the poet Clovis Hesteau de Nuysement, in the former, and another entitled Du Feu et du Sel (Of fire and salt) in the latter. Ms. Français 2535 contains, for its part, Advis sur le salpestre (Advice on saltpeter).

Comparisons with Ms. Fr. 640

Ms. Fr. 640 contains very few entries relating to alchemy and no entries relating to the art of discourse—two themes characterizing the Béthune manuscripts—but many manuscripts in the collection, which treat these themes echo the interests of the author-practitioner. For example, among writings dealing with eloquence and the art of discourse are recipes for inscribing letters on paper or in stone, providing a connection between the theory of discourse and applied writing. A folio in Ms. Français 2587 provides the means “to remove letters from paper,” “to remove ink stains,” and “to put a letter in a stone.”17 However, a comparison shows little overlap between this recipe and that for “Removing stains” in Ms. Fr. 640 (fol. 29v). The entry in Ms. Fr. 640 relates to stains on clothing and its main ingredient is spirits, which do not appear in the recipes of Ms. Français 2587. Similarly, the recipe for “Erasing a letter” (fol. 46v) recommends the use of sulfur oil, which also does not appear in Ms. Français 2587. The recipes entitled “On stone, black letters” (fol. 46v) in Ms. Fr. 640 and “To put a letter in a stone” in Ms. Français 2587 are also completely different.

Among the manuscripts dealing with metals and ingredients also mentioned by the author-practitioner, Ms. Français 2535, which contains an Advis sur le salpestre, resonates with the author-practitioner’s use of saltpeter.18 Thus, both authors (whose handwriting is different) point out, for example, that saltpeter is found along walls (fol. 14r).19 However, in the author-practitioner’s frequent use of saltpeter (fols. 37r, 39r, 48v, 70r, 81v, 92v, 100r–101r, 102r, 106r, 116v, 120r–120v, 123r–123v, 134v–135r, 136v, 154r), no further parallels between Ms. Français 2535 and Ms. Fr. 640 can be found.

Other themes mentioned in Ms. Fr. 640 are found sporadically in this set of manuscripts. For example, in the course of a discussion about the categories that make up the natural world, Ms. Français 2516, gives general information about animals, but does not treat the subject in any way close to that of the author-practitioner.20 Often mixed with excerpts on rhetoric, philosophy, and alchemy, texts on medicine and anatomy are also found in several manuscripts.21 In addition, three manuscripts deal in part with magic.22 Ms. Français 2542 contains Extraict d’un proces fait à un magicien de Moulins dated 1623, while Ms. Français 2555 contains a text entitled De l’Appologie des grandsz personnages tenuz pour magiciens. Although neither of these manuscripts contain sleight of hand tricks, and thus do not compare with those in Ms. Fr. 640, the subjects are related and may have been of interest to the Béthunes.23

Ms. Français 2529: Comparison to Ms. Fr. 640

Ms. Français 2529 shows similarities with Ms. Fr. 640 in its themes and its practical approach. This manuscript begins with notes on metals divided into two chapters entitled “Grand œuvre” (“Great work”) and “Generation des metaux” (“Generation of metals”) (Fig. 1).24 General information about the properties of metals is provided, but these folios do not present recipes or observations that could link this manuscript to Ms. Fr. 640. After leaving several folios blank, the author indicates his name, “M. Bossard,” and addresses, in a similar handwriting to the preceding one (see Fig. 1), a text entitled “De la Chymie” (“On chemistry”) by explaining “Sir, hereby I satisfy your description of me.”25 In it, he presents his reflections on alchemy and on what he calls “the great masterpiece of art,” explaining that this knowledge is the result of personal experience: “it is in a few words the fruit of 20 years of study and mediocre practice that I offer you with as much good heart as I want to be believed,” signing “your very humble and obedient servant, Bossard.”26 It is not possible to ascertain if the author is addressing Philippe or Hippolyte de Béthune.

Further on, there is a short recipe which Bossard borrows from a certain “M. du Rochat,” describing how to dye “sel nitre” by adding gold, lime, and coral, without providing specific instructions.27 Bossard then records a series of entries on mines, plants, and animals.28 While these excerpts are not intended to provide recipes or instructions, instead presenting general knowledge, the author nevertheless shows interests similar to those of the author-practitioner of Ms. Fr. 640. Similarly, several chapters entitled “Des causes des maladies” (Causes of disease), “Preceptes de santé” (Precepts of health), and “De la Santé du prince” (Of the health of the prince) deal with health and medicine.29 Finally, the theme of magic is also present, with an extract from Jean Bodin’s De la Demonomanie de sorciers.30

This manuscript also contains excerpts from texts such as Plato’s Timaeus, Tertullian’s Mantle, and the works of Hermes Trismegistus. It thus deals with a large number of themes, comprising the knowledge gathered over twenty years of study and practice by a man who has perhaps carried out experiments. In this way, it represents interests and experience similar to that of the author-practitioner, with the difference that Bossard presents his knowledge in an orderly manner, classifying it by themes, whereas the author-practitioner provides recipes in which all these fields of interest are interspersed. Bossard also includes excerpts from scholarly texts, which Ms. Fr. 640 does not.

Conclusion

The themes covered in the set of manuscripts Français 2513-2589 are rather varied and testify to the diversity of the Béthunes’ areas of interest. Among these manuscripts, several include epistles and texts on various subjects related to history, politics, finance, morality, and astrology,31 and the art of discourse and alchemy are strongly represented.

In this entire ensemble, one manuscript in particular—Ms. Français 2529—testifies to the observations and experiences of an author named Bossard which are comparable to those of the author-practitioner. Although this manuscript (the handwriting of which is different from that of Ms. Fr. 640) cannot be directly linked to Ms. Fr. 640, it nevertheless presents the example of an author whose profile is comparable to that of the author-practitioner. Thus, this manuscript, a part of which is addressed to “Monsieur,” could be an instance of a work commissioned from an expert practitioner by a nobleman such as Philippe or Hippolyte de Béthune.
