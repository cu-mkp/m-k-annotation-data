Polishing and Engraving Stones

Angel Jiang

Introduction

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains thirty-two entries on the treatment of stones and gemstones (see Appendix at the end of this essay). They range in tone and purpose, from brief statements that identify appropriate polishing grits to detailed instructions for the creation of “counterfeit” gemstones.1 Of those thirty-two, twenty-three involve gemstones (pierreries) and amount to an informal collection of entries on gems and their treatments. Engraved gems have been worn and collected as objects invested with personal attachment and mystical properties since antiquity.2 Although discussion of gems persisted through the early modern period in popular texts on healing and alchemy, and were in some cases accepted as useful in medical practice, the author-practitioner does not mention these uses.3 Instead, a loose cluster of entries (fols. 8v, 10r, 11v, and 13r) details the working of gems and metals for decorative purposes; this essay focuses on two of those entries, “For Diamond Points for Engraving” (fol. 11v) and “Polishing of Stones” (fol. 13r). The former appears on a folio with three other short recipes, two of which concern the decoration of steel and gold, and the latter shares its page with instructions on pewtering tin and calcining gold. These two entries help locate the manuscript’s entries on gemstones within the art of the goldsmith, whose expertise encompassed precious stones and metals.4

The author-practitioner’s interest in the lapidary arts should first be contextualized within the expansion of the trade. In the first place, a rise in the collecting of precious stones was tied to global trade, since the introduction of emeralds, rubies, and diamonds into European markets increased the number and quality of stones available.5 In Venice, availability coupled with increasing interest in natural history cultivated fascination with gems both as objets d’art and natural materials.6 In Florence, the collections of the Medici boasted an unprecedented number of cameos and intaglios.7 Collectors prized gemstones for their hardness as well as the related properties of color and clarity.8 Special value was reserved for the hardest of precious stones, diamonds and corundums such as rubies and sapphires, due to their ability to hold a shine and resist tools.9

Texts describing new tools and techniques accompanied collectors’ growing interest in gems. A key example is the Konst grafwa stena (The Art of Engraving Stones), a text Marjolijn Bol considers emblematic of the state of the art of the lapidary in the early sixteenth century.10 The basic standards of judgement and materials used to enhance the physical properties of gems have remained remarkably consistent.11 A stone’s hardness is directly proportional to the amount of polish it can receive: harder stones can hold higher polish—something discussed by sixteenth-century authors.12 In his text, Peder Månsson (d. 1534) describes the use of a continuous rotary motion lathe as opposed to the earlier reciprocal motion lathe for work on hard gemstones.13 When coupled with the appropriate abrasives, this machine facilitated the shaping and polishing of the hardest and therefore most precious of gems with relative speed and ease.14

“Diamond Points for Engraving” (fol. 11v)

Tools, access to stones, and practical knowledge framed a goldsmith’s practices. Consideration of the tools, machines, grits, and precious stones that appear in Ms. Fr. 640 therefore helps clarify the extent of the author-practitioner’s knowledge and practice of the lapidary arts. “For Diamond Points for Engraving” (fol. 11v) and “Polishing and Engraving Stones” (fol. 13r) concern the preparation and polishing of engraved stones specifically, or stones bearing designs carved in relief. In “For Diamond Points for Engraving” (fol. 11v) the author-practitioner mentions two tools commonly used to engrave and polish stones: diamond points and a wheel.

The first, diamond points, are used to “trace” (trasser) what one wants to “draw” (desseigner) on a stone. Diamond is the hardest stone; as such, it can cut easily any other stone but can be cut only by another diamond.15 Diamond-tipped scribers or styluses have been the tools of choice for incising designs on stones since antiquity. According to Pliny, engravers sought out shards of diamonds broken “into splinters” because they could be inserted into iron tools and used to cut the hardest of materials with little effort.16 Medieval and early modern artisans continued to use diamond points for this same purpose. The fourteenth-century Doctrina Poliendi pretiosos lapides, for example, includes an entry “for engraving stones” (pour graver les pierres) that entails the use of a stylus with a diamond point.17

While the basic principle of abrading a stone with harder grits persisted from antiquity to the early modern period, in the sixteenth century the use of rotary machines expedited processes of polishing, shaping, and engraving. By the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, rotary technologies were used for all lapidary techniques (Fig. 1).18 The author-practitioner does mention a wheel in “For Diamond Points for Engraving,” but not for the purpose of tracing or engraving. He instead counsels to “unpolish” (despolir) stones on a lead wheel. His instruction to “unpolish on a lead wheel” wheel contains two significant word choices—one relates to technique and tool, the other to material and texture.

First, the author-practitioner organizes his process within the paradigm of fat and lean.19 Diamond points must be rubbed against each other if they are “fatty” (grasses) and “cannot bite” (ne peuvent mordre); stones similarly have their “fatty” quality diminished before being incised. “Fatty,” a term used by sixteenth-century artisans, miners, and scholars, describes earth and stone by analogy to the richness of agricultural land, or the properties of clay.20 Biringuccio, for example, writes that the use of lean clay in a crucible made it more resistant to fire; lean metal for casting in bronze was more brittle but resistant to fire, while fattiness gave metal more malleability.21 In “Diamond points for engraving,” the term “fatty” can be seen to refer to the texture of a polished stone. Polishing entails the gradual removal of the stone’s surface in a progression from coarse to fine scratches until they are imperceptible to the human eye and hand. At that point, the eye perceives a gloss and the stone feels slick to the touch.22 The same “fatty” quality of a polished stone, however, prevents diamond points from “biting” on its surface. The author-practitioner therefore recommends that one decrease the fattiness of the stone to help the diamond point grab onto its surface. In his instruction to “unpolish” (despolir) the stone, he refers to the removal of a gloss achieved by polishing, which is perceived as fatty.23 The author-practitioner’s final remark on the “fatty” quality of a fingernail alludes to an artisan’s use of his body as a tool of quality control: because artisans commonly tested the sharpness of their tools against their fingernails, the author-practitioner references this practice to explain and justify why both tool and stone should not be “fatty.”24 This remark, which can be understood within a broad artisanal epistemology rather than one specific to goldsmiths, communicates the desired texture of a stone for tracing.

Second, the author-practitioner describes the use of diamond points set in an iron stylus, and not a rotary drill or lathe, for “tracing” a desired design on a stone. Though the author-practitioner’s ambiguous description does not exclude the use of a rotary machine for tracing, the “diamond points” in this entry more likely to refer to a scriber. The use of diamond points to “trace” rather than “engrave” supports this hypothesis.25 Tracing (trasser) on stone with a scriber and engraving (graver) with a lathe signify distinct techniques and produce entirely different results. In the manuscript “trace” is used to refer to transferring images (fol. 42v, 46v, 39v) or going over lines (102v, 163r, 37v, 152v). “Tracing” does not necessarily imply a discernible removal of matter from the stone’s surface as “engraving” does.

For my reconstruction of “For diamond points for engraving,” I used a diamond-tipped scriber to trace a design onto a polished amethyst (Fig. 2). I selected a translucent amethyst with one flat side to eliminate the necessity of shaping a workable side, since this would have required the use of a lapidary wheel (Fig. 3). Without a wheel, I also could not “unpolish” the stone as the author-practitioner suggests. Though I did not remove the stone of its polish by roughening its surface, the diamond-tipped scriber still scratched the surface of the amethyst easily, yielding a faint but legible design after several “tracings” (Fig. 4). Since the diamond points bit well on a polished stone, the instruction to “unpolish” or “roughen” a stone in order to facilitate tracing remains ambiguous. The distinction between “trace” and “engrave,” on the other hand, was clear and appropriate for this entry: tracing a design with a diamond-tipped scribe resulted in a removal of material from the stone’s surface visible to the eye but imperceptible to the touch as relief (Fig. 5).26 According to the entry, this traced stone would have been ready for the implied step of engraving.

Although the title and the implied preparatory step prior to tracing suggest this entry is about engraving stones, engraving techniques and instruments are conspicuously absent. “Engrave” (graver) is used in Ms. Fr. 640 across media, namely to work wood, iron, stone, copper, and glass, and it refers most certainly in this entry to the engraving of gemstones. However, the author-practitioner never mentions “engraved gemstones” as a category of object, or the use of a lathe, the tool most commonly used to engrave gemstones.27 The engraved gemstone is instead present in the manuscript as an implied object in “For Diamond Points for Engraving” (fol. 11v) and a finished object in “Polishing Engraved Stone” (fol. 13r).

“Polishing Engraved Stones” (fol. 13r)

If “For Diamond Points for Engraving” (fol. 11v) prepares a stone for engraving, “Polishing Engraved Stones” (fol. 13r) supplies instructions for the polishing of an engraved stone. As mentioned, the key intermediate step between these two—the engraving of the stone—is absent. I chose to engrave a stone in this reconstruction for several reasons. Polishing a freshly engraved stone might help clarify whether or not the “Polishing Engraved Stones” entry was intended for a newly engraved stone, presumably executed by the author-practitioner himself, or an already-engraved stone acquired by the author-practitioner.

As described in De natura fossilium of 1546 by Georgius Agricola (1494–1555), gem engravers used rotary tools consisting of a diamond-tip set in an iron stylus placed in a shaft. A heavy cord is wrapped around this shaft and attached to a wheel. The engraver turns the wheel with the right hand and, while rotating the shaft, holds the stone against this stylus with the left hand.28 Accordingly, it is difficult to follow with a lathe the design traced with a diamond point on a gemstone. While tracing on stone is done much like drawing, engraving requires one to hold the stone against a machine, moving it in slight motions. The author-practitioner was clearly aware of the use of lathes, as he refers to goldsmiths using this machine to make wooden forms for “grand works” in a different entry.29 The lack of a lathe for engraving in Ms. Fr. 640 suggests that the author-practitioner did not observe or engrave his gems, which may in fact be consistent with period practices. By the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, artisans began to specialize in either gem engraving or gem cutting; the former was reserved for select goldsmiths.30

For the purpose of reconstructing “Polishing Engraved Stones” (fol. 13r), I engraved the amethyst with diamond-tipped burrs installed in modern lapidary equipment (Fig. 6). All polishing and engraving requires the mixing of abrasives in water or oil, which act as a coolant and lubricant.31 On contact with the water, the shallow design I traced with a diamond-point disappeared (Fig. 7). Similarly, engraving with a slurry of grit and oil or water, as gem engravers traditionally did, would render the design invisible by obscuring the surface of the stone.32 Art biographer and theorist Giorgio Vasari (1511–1574) fittingly described the art of engraving in intaglio as “working in the dark.”33 The engraved stone that resulted from my reconstruction bore a legible intaglio design (Fig. 8). Observation of its scratched surface under a macro lens reveals its roughness; newly engraved stones require another polish to restore them to a shine (Fig. 9). The instruction to “trace what you want to draw on stone” coupled with the absence of observations or records of the engraving process itself suggest the delegation of this step to a specialist.

Though it is unlikely that the author-practitioner engraved precious stones, he was plausibly familiar with techniques of polishing and setting. He refers to using gemstone backings in ways that suggest their presence in his workshop, invoking burnishing techniques as a point of reference and repurposing the backings for other projects.34 There are in fact two entries in Ms. Fr. 640 dedicated to polishing stones. In “Polishing of Stones” (fol. 8v), the author-practitioner refers to the use of a hierarchy of tools depending on the hardness of the stone. Amethyst, emerald, and “the more tender stones” are polished on the tin wheel, whereas diamond, ruby, sapphire, and oriental jacinth, all grouped together in this entry as stones of a similar hardness, are commonly cut or polished on the copper wheel. Since diamonds, sapphires, and corundum are all above 9 on the Mohs scale and are at least 400 times harder than amethyst, which is a 7 on the Mohs scale, amethyst is indeed a “tender stone” by comparison; the author-practitioner’s suggestions are consistent with period and modern knowledge of the relative hardness of these stones.35 The instruction to use a copper wheel for precious stones is also consistent with period practices.36

The use of tin, lead, or copper wheels, however, is irrelevant to the hardness of the stone. Abrasives determine the lapidary’s ability to polish hard stones rather than the metal of the wheels such abrasives are applied to. In the case of “tender stones,” emery or diamond should still be used.37 While Agricola and Mansson both describe the use of copper wheels, which would make plausible the remark on fol. 8v that precious stones should be polished on said wheel, they specify the type of grit.38 The explicit hierarchy among lead, tin, and copper wheels and the lack of specificity about a polishing grit raises an inconsistency between Ms. Fr. 640 and contemporaneous lapidary texts. This reflects either a lack of knowledge on the author-practitioner’s part, or simply that he did not feel it necessary to record. There are reasons to see the latter possibility as likely.

Other entries on gemstones and metalwork support the hypothesis that the author-practitioner possessed the knowledge of the appropriate grit. The author-practitioner observed gem cutting and shaping, as suggested by his entry “Sapphire” (fol. 38r), in which he describes stonecutters counterfeiting sapphires with antique glass. In order to do so, “they choose beautiful blue” and “cut it in squares with emery.”39 The use of “they” and his further details of their cutting “in bevel” and subsequent polishing suggest that the author-practitioner was aware of the grit needed to cut antique glass, let alone harder stones.

The author-practitioner details the need for a specific grit in his second entry on polishing. “Polishing Engraved Stones” (fol. 13r) refers to a point in an engraved stone’s lifecycle after its cutting, shaping, and polishing. “Flat” and “faceted” stones are polished on a copper wheel, with no mention of an accompanying grit. Engraved stones, on the other hand, should be polished not on the copper wheel, but with “brushes and tripoli.” Tripoli is a common material in Ms. Fr. 640 and in early modern lapidary workshops. Agricola describes the use of tripoli as the first abrasive agent used to polish a gem: it is an earth “sprinkled on a lead wheel which is rotated with the right hand while the gem is held firmly against it with the left.”40 Unlike Agricola, the author-practitioner makes no mention of a device such as a wooden spindle set with pitch for holding the stone while polishing or the use of a wheel, both of which would have facilitated the process.41 To reconstruct this entry, I used two types of tripoli of different particle sizes along with a stiff hog-hair brush, and held the stone in my hand (Vid. 1).

The tripoli and brushes accomplished nothing perceptible to either touch or sight, even at 20x magnification (Fig. 10). The scratches on the stone’s relief appear consistent both before and after polishing. Polishing by hand is a laborious and time-intensive process, and I polished for only an hour. It is unsurprising, then, that the results were imperceptible. Based on consultation with an expert jeweler, however, polishing with tripoli as a grit charged on a wheel—let alone manually with brushes—is unlikely to make any difference on a newly engraved stone.42 Polishing is conducted in a progression from grits of coarser to increasingly finer particle size. Without using intermediary grits, tripoli may have been far too fine a polishing agent to use on a newly engraved stone. It may also be that the diamond grit engraving bits I used for the reconstruction were too coarse and left the gem in a comparatively rougher state than sixteenth-century tools might have.

The use of tripoli and brushes, which were both readily available in the workshop and used at the time, may have been sufficient to polish engraved stones to higher shine, rather than polish a particularly rough, newly-engraved surface to a base level of shine. Tripoli’s lack of effect on the amethyst may illuminate more precisely the state of the stone referenced in this entry. Brushes and tripoli were likely not intended to be used on a stone engraved personally by the author-practitioner. Instead, they may indicate the author-practitioner’s recording of received knowledge about polishing techniques or possession of an already engraved cameo or intaglio, the polish of which he desired to further refine.

Though the author-practitioner mentions the need for tripoli and brushes, Mansson notes that the brushes are to be set in a lathe—a tool the author-practitioner does not mention. The author-practitioner’s “brushes and tripoli” might imply the use of a lathe, but this would amount to another instance in which knowledge is assumed. His instruction to use “brushes and tripoli” instead of the “said wheel” of copper in this entry indicates his understanding of the logic behind each of their uses: flat and faceted stones can be polished on a wheel because their surfaces meet the plane of the wheel, whereas engraved stones require brushes to reach grooves carved in relief. The author-practitioner was likely familiar with techniques specific to the latter, but not personally equipped to execute these tasks.

The author-practitioner’s level of expertise as knowledgeable about but not himself practicing gem engraving opens up other possibilities as to the role of gemstones in his workshop. Artists were known to have sourced precious gems for their patrons, in which case a final polishing of tripoli would have simply finished their shine.43 The author-practitioner may have acquired or commissioned engraved gems for a patron to be mounted in marvelous ensembles for a kunstkammer and to be used in jewelry.44 Examples of ancient intaglios and early modern imitations set in objects and jewelry abound (Fig. 11). The manuscript’s entries on polishing and engraving stones reveal insights into the author-practitioner’s encounters with and knowledge of gems and their treatments, and allude to their potential afterlives in a wide array of settings.

Certain others stubbornly resist efforts to engrave them, for example, Indian diamond, Scythian and Egyptian smaragdus, sapphirus, sapphire and the carbunculus which holds part of the wax in the signet.” Agricola, De natura fossilium, Book VI (1955), 114.↩︎
