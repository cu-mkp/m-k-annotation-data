Artisans of Toulouse

Sarah Muñoz and Colin Debuiche

Goldsmiths of Toulouse

The “Comptes du trésorier,” the “Pièces à l’appui des comptes,” and the cadastres in the Archives municipales of Toulouse report many names of goldsmiths. Among these goldsmiths, two can be distinguished because they worked for the Académie des Jeux floraux (the Floral Games Academy). The flowers cast in gold and silver given to winners of the poetry competition of the Jeux floraux were made by the goldsmith Jérôme Véret from 1576 to 1582, then by the goldsmith Arnaud Cornayre in 1583, and finally by Jérôme Véret again from 1584 to 1589. Although no definitive link between their activity and Ms. Fr. 640 is proven, the manuscript does contain many recipes relating to the molding and life casting of plants, including fols. 106r, 115v, 116r, 116v, 117r, 120r, 122r, 129r, 145v, 154v, 155r–v, 157r, 159v, 160r. Artisans of the Toulouse Mint

A very large number of recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 concern molding, casting, varnishing, and repairing of medals. In addition, the author-practitioner explicitly mentions the creation of medals, and he made several “medailles de relief” (fols. 69r, 156r) and molded “une grande piece d’un portraict de Jesus” using sand from Toulouse (fol. 85v). We therefore searched for the names of the engravers of the Toulouse Mint. The “Comptes du trésorier,” the “Pièces à l’appui des comptes,” the cadastres, and the registers of the notary Massiot (which contain transactions involving some masters of the Mint) report the names of several people working at the Toulouse Mint.

Masters of the Arsenal

The “Comptes du trésorier,” the “Pièces à l’appui des comptes,” and the cadastres provide the names of gunners, cannon makers, armor makers, founders of cannon balls, and powder makers, who worked for the Arsenal or who were paid because they repaired or cleaned the artillery pieces.

Among the powder makers, Antoine Subreville, Pierre Fontanges, Vital Pelletier, and Martin Limozin rented an iron forge located in the quarter of Saint-Cyprien to cast artillery pieces and cannon balls (Archives municipales de Toulouse, CC1988, f°21). Furthermore, the leader of the artillery of Toulouse was Jean Gout, “bourgeois de Toulouse,” “capitaine,” and “commissaire” of the artillery (Archives municipales de Toulouse, CC1987, f°19–19v° ; CC1988, f°47).

Other Metalworkers of the Arsenal

Other artisans were paid by the “capitouls” for making works in metal. The most important and frequently mentioned metalworkers in the “Comptes du trésorier” and the “Pièces à l’appui des comptes” were the “fèvres grossiers,” who made objects with large dimensions, and the “rodiers,” who made wheels (especially for the cannons and the “couleuvrines” of the Arsenal).

Other Artisans of Toulouse

As many recipes in Ms. Fr. 640 concern other materials and techniques, painters and artisans working with woad or sand, employed by the “capitouls” between 1570 and 1590, were collected.

The profession of molder (“mouleur”) did not exist in Toulouse in the sixteenth century. This profession is noted in the statutes of Paris established in 1572, but the word “mouleur” never appears in the archives of Toulouse. In Ms. Fr. 640, “Le mouleur de th{ou}l{ous}e” is mentioned on fol. 69r.
