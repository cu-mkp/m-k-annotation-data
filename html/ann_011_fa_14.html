“Eau Magistra:” Investigating Binders for Sand Molds

Emogene Cataldo and Julianna Van Visco

Sands and Binders

BnF Ms. Fr. 640 contains many entries that describe sand-casting processes, both for making sand molds and for casting into sand molds. To make a successful mold, a dry “sand” and a wet binding agent are combined to create a mixture that is able to take and hold an impression, as well as withstand the heat and pressure of a molten material.

A “sand” can be made from a variety of materials. The author-practitioner mentions, among many options, calcined ox hooves, pulverized sand, and burned felt, as ingredients in sand molds.1 Ms. Fr. 640 also cites diverse materials that may be used as “wet” binding agents, which are often called “Magistry” or magistra in early modern craft recipes. In Pirotechnia, Biringuccio provides a recipe for making a magistry of salt and water: “Because it is necessary that the powders made for casting have a magistery of salt water, I wish now to teach you to prepare the salt that must form this magistery, for without it these powders would not have the toughness to hold themselves together when they dry.”2 Hugh Plat also uses this term in an entry in his 1594 Jewel house of art and nature, but not in the context of molding or casting processes.3

Magistra

What is the meaning of “magistra,” and why might it be used in these binder recipes? Do these binders have special properties that would merit the term “magistry,” or does the word have multiple meanings? Moreover, what are these binders, are they all alike, or can some be called “magisterial,” and others not? By considering the materials used in Ms. Fr. 640’s mold-making recipes and by investigating the materials themselves, it is possible to understand why one binding agent might be preferable to another. For example, egg whites as a binder may give a different strength than wine and elm root decoction.

The French term magistra is derived from the Latin word magisterium, used in Classical Latin to refer to the office of a magistrate, and sometimes used later in medieval Latin to refer to the philosophers’ stone.4 The term magistra does not appear in Randle Cotgrave’s 1611 French-English dictionary, although he defines a related word magistère as “Maistership, authoritie, sway, cheefe rule, also, maisterie, a maisters part, or maisterpeece.”5 This entry is followed by several related terms that may relate to the author-practitioner’s use of magistra: the adjective magistral implies skilled mastery: “Magistrall; of a maister, or magistrate, or maister; also, maister-like; artificiall, skilfull, cunning.”6 The adverb magistralement similarly connotes skill: “Maister-like, expertly, artificially, cunningly.”7 Turning to English usage, the Oxford English Dictionary defines “Magistry” as having chymical import, with most usage of the word in seventeenth-century texts.8 Magistry can mean a “master principle of nature, free of impurities...a substance, such as the philosopher’s stone, capable of transmuting or changing the nature of other substances,” but can also denote the “residuum obtained by an acid solution; a precipitate; a resinous extract.”9 In alchemical texts, the magistery or magisterium can refer to the great work, the opus magnum, or the attainment of the philosophers’ stone by which the corruptions of both humans and metals could be rectified and brought to a higher state of health. In Archidoxis, Paracelsus writes, “This therefore is a Magistery, viz that which can be Extracted out of things without any separation or Preparation of the Elements, and yet notwithstanding, the Powers and virtues of things, are by the addition of some thing, attracted into that matter and conserved there.”10 While magistra, or magistery, possesses several meanings, in Ms. Fr. 640, it would seem to have two dimensions: first, as a masterpiece of skill, and, second, as a substance that can effect material transformation, for example, in Ms. Fr. 640, “eau magistra” can render a dry substance moist, or “tempered,” and a lean substance fat, or unctuous.

In a recipe for casting on fol. 68r, the author-practitioner specifies that a sand “releases very neatly, does not want to be moistened with magistra or anything else, but needs to be completely dry, in its natural state, finely pulverized.” Because both wine and “well-beaten glair of egg” (i.e., egg whites) are mentioned on fol. 84v as substances with which to moisten sands, Magistra would seem in contrast to indicate a mixture. Moreover, on fol. 69r, the author-practitioner specifies wine or magistra, and magistra or egg glair. Thus, the author-practitioner differentiates here between magistra and binding agents that are not mixtures of two or more ingredients. Indeed, on fol. 84v, salt and water, and wine and elm root are named as magistras.

On fol. 84v, elm roots boiled in wine are given as a better alternative to salt water, which “some people” think is “not good.” This mixture is also mentioned on fols. 69r, 72r, 85v, and 87v. On fol. 69v, it forms a remedy for “lean sands;” on fol 72r, it is used for molds in which copper will be cast; and, on 86r, where the author-practitioner recounts his experiments with sands, he gives egg glair as an alternative to the wine and elm root mixture: “Since then I molded the bone of oxen feet, burned, pulverized, & sieved through a double sieve & hum fort moistened with glair of egg or wine boiled with elm root.”

Making eau magistra

Both “Eau Magistra” on fol. 84v and “Magistra” on fol. 87v describe how the decoction should be made: “Founders take the roots of a young elm when it is in sap, & boil it in wine, or better yet vinegar, and keep it all year long in a barrel” (fol. 87v). There are several interesting points to note about this entry: first, the author-practitioner writes about founders in the third person, possibly excluding himself from this group; second, the fact that the magistra is made in large quantities and stored, indicating that the time of year and the rising of the sap in the tree is important; and, third, that wine and vinegar are apparently equivalent for making this decoction.

In our experiments to reconstruct different magistras, we were able to make a substance that performed well in sand-casting and that behaved similarly to egg white, or glair. We set out to follow the recipe, a seemingly straightforward process, as the manuscript simply instructs to boil wine with elm root. Nevertheless, we encountered various obstacles in its recreation. To begin, we chose an inexpensive Cabernet Sauvignon, given the apparent equivalence between wine and vinegar. We were unable to harvest elm root when the sap was rising, instead using slippery elm inner bark powder, which is still used in CAM (Complementary and Alternative Medicine) recipes.11 We substituted this for the freshly harvested elm root.12 The recipe did not specify the quantity of elm root, and, with a scarcity of early modern sources on this point,13 we consulted modern medicinal recipes to determine the quantity. Boiling one cup of wine with two teaspoons of powdered slippery elm bark resulted in a viscous liquid that smelled of fruit and had the thick, mucilaginous texture of egg whites. The substance coheres, forming globules much like the mucous-like character of egg whites, but with a more uneven consistency.14 (Fig. 1)

Glair of egg

As discussed above, egg whites were also used as a binding agent and are mentioned in several recipes in Ms. Fr. 640;15 however it is unclear if the author-practitioner considered egg whites to be a magistra, or, as his aforementioned account of sand experiments on fol. 85v implies, it was an alternative to using magistra.16 In the entry “Glair of eggs” (fol. 82r) the author-practitioner suggests that its particular advantage as a binding agent is its strength: “Glair of eggs gives strength to sand to make several casts.” Other entries in Ms. Fr. 640 use egg glair for molding and casting: on fol. 83r, in “Another sand,” the author-practitioner recommends mixing well-beaten egg glair with clay earth and vine charcoal, calcining the mixture, then tempering it with vinegar. In the entry for “Sand for lead, the most excellent of all, for high and low reliefs”on fol. 84v, egg glair is used to moisten ceruse (lead white) for use in a mold. On fol. 85v in “Founder,” egg glair is mixed with earth in making the mold for bells. The recipe “Excellent sand for lead, tin and copper” on fol. 86v, which mentions many materials for sand molds, including burned bone, iron dross, and burned felt, uses egg glair as a binder, and reportedly enabled more than one cast in the mold.

The Squeeze Test

We tested both egg whites and the elm root and wine decoction as binders for sand molding in frames. Our sand was made of previously-used molds (which had been made from 1:1 crushed brick and plaster of Paris).17 While they had small differences, the sand mixture with egg glair and that with elm root decoction were very similar to one another in tactile handfeel properties. When mixed with the sand, the consistency for both mixtures was almost more oily than wet, and the sand clumped like moist brown sugar rather than a soft, wet clay. It was easy to tell when the consistency of the sand made it ready for the molds: we used a “squeeze test” described in “Casting in frames” on fol. 118v: the sand should give “a good hold, nevertheless coming apart easily” (faisoict bonne prise sesmiant touteffois aisement). The importance of this expression, which is used several times in the manuscript, about how to determine the correct liquid content and consistency of molding sand became clear to us as we worked. In order to make this test, we took a handful of the sand mixture and squeezed it into the palm. If it held together, but could also be easily broken apart by applying pressure to it with a fingertip, it was ready for molding. (Fig. 2, Fig. 3) This was in contrast to another sand we made using ammonium chloride and brandy as a binder, in which the sand turned out muddy and wet, very different from when we used egg glair or the elm root-wine decoction. (Fig. 4) The two sands made with egg glair and elm root-wine decoction packed exceptionally well into the frame molds. They did not seem too moist or too dry, and both produced detailed impressions.18 (Fig. 5, Fig. 6) Just as the recipes in the manuscript indicate, the mold made with egg white was strong enough to endure two castings of molten tin (and could very possibly have cast a third).19 (Fig. 7) The mold made with the elm root eau magistra produced a fine product, but the mold did not hold up well enough to produce two castings.20 (Fig. 8)

Conclusion

In conclusion, our research shows the author-practitioner’s interest in experimenting with different types of binders, including magistra. All these binders, whether labelled as magistra or not, played a crucial role in transforming substances from one state to another in order to produce new properties: in this case, to create a sand of just the right consistency that would withstand the casting of molten metal. Our experiments with egg glair replicated the statements in Ms. Fr. 640 about the strength of this binding agent and its ability to withstand multiple castings. The magistra made with elm root and wine also performed well, and might have had the added benefit of being able to be made in large quantities and stored for long periods of time.
